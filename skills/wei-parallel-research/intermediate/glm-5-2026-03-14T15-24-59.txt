=== Summary ===
Quantum computing is a type of computation that harnesses the principles of quantum mechanics—specifically superposition and entanglement—to process information. Unlike classical computers, which use bits representing either a 0 or a 1, quantum computers use quantum bits (qubits) that can represent and store a combination of both states simultaneously. This architecture allows quantum computers to solve specific complex problems, such as cryptography and molecular simulation, much faster than classical supercomputers.

=== Key Points ===
- **Fundamental Unit:** Uses qubits instead of classical bits; qubits can exist in a state of superposition (both 0 and 1 at the same time).
- **Core Mechanics:** Relies on quantum entanglement, where qubits become interconnected and the state of one instantly influences another, allowing for complex parallel processing.
- **Applications:** Shows potential for massive speedups in specific fields, including drug discovery, materials science, optimization problems, and breaking current encryption standards.
- **Current Status:** Technology is in the Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum (NISQ) era; hardware is currently limited by error rates and the difficulty of maintaining quantum coherence.
